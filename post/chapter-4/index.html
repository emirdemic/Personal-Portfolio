Testing the Explanation for Phonological Similarity Effect

July 31, 2019

Phonological similarity effect refers to an observation that the recall of phonologically similar letters is worse than of those which are not similar. The effect was tested in multiple settings and seems to be highly robust. One potential explanation which was not extensively tested is that similar letters do not have discriminative retrieval cues, which leads to their poor recall. In this experiment, co-authored by Ana Avramović and Milica Šimonović and presented in Belgrade and Cambridge, the explanation was put to a direct test.

Research Goal

Note: The poster with more details is available here, while the full notebook with code will soon be available online.

Imagine someone tells you to remember the following sequence of letters: BDCEP. It should come as no surprise that recall of these letters is going to be harder than if our task was to remember a sequence such as KTRSV. This observation is in psychology referred to as a phonological similarity effect.

However, observing a fenomenon should be followed by offering its explanation. One explanation was proposed by Crossman (1968) and was further explored by Alan Baddeley (1968). This explanation states that recall is worse since phonologically similar letters do not have highly discriminative retrieval cues. If you are asking yourself what retrieval cues are, you can think of any event from your past and you can see that remembering one detail leads to remembering another one. In esential, the first detail you remembered is a retrieval cue for the second one. Similarly, when you remember a sequence such as SRFTP, recalling letter S leads to a recall of the next one, and so on. However, let us examine the sequence KBRCFP. In this sequence, B, C, and P are similar letters, which means that retrieval cues are not going to be of much use. In esential, if you remember letter R, you would have hard time remembering if that letter is a retrieval cue for B, C, or P.

Even though the explanation makes perfect sense, it was not extensively checked. Therefore, the goal of this research was to check the explanation and overcome methodological issues of the original Baddeley’s experiment.

Results

The experiment was conducted at University of Belgrade with a total of 18 participants. Their task was to remember different sets of similar and dissimilar letters. Analyzing collected data suggested that the explanation in terms of retrieval cues holds. There was one deviation from the original study which does not pose a problem for the explanation itself, but should be further examined in future research.

Achievements and Dissemination

Results of the experiment were presented at the conference 25th Empirical Studies in Psychology held annually in Belgrade and the authors were awarded for the best poster-presented research. The experiment was also presented at jConference in Cambridge and conference LEPovanje organized by Laboratory for Experimental Psychology.
